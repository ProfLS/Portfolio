Discrimination in Airport Security

This site summarizes my research project examining unequal treatment of South Asians and other minorities at U.S. airports, combining a brief literature review with a small original survey and concrete steps you can take to report issues and push for accountability. My goal is to ensure that property steps are taken by Congress to prevent discrimination during airport security, alongside providing resources to report any occurences.

The Problem

South Asians and other minorities report disproportionate targeting at airport checkpoints, often described as “flying while brown.” Beyond inconvenience, repeated secondary screenings and profiling can harm mental health and erode civic trust. Policy choices since 2001 have also shifted the balance between security and accountability, sometimes weakening avenues for redress. Remember, you have rights.

What the literature says

  • Mental Health Impacts: Discrimination by security personnel is linked to psychosomatic health issues and reduced trust among immigrant youth.
  • Policy tradeoffs: Post 9/11 authorities expanded TSA powers that can override some civil rights protections, complicating accountability. Many attempts to implement transparent procedures are discretionary and fall flat.
  • Training matters: Consistent, targeted training improves screening performance by supporting investments in anti‑bias and procedure training early in careers.
Original Survey

Sample & Method

We surveyed 15 people (10 South Asian; 3 Caucasian; 2 East Asian) using a 10 item Likert questionnaire on perceptions of TSA/airport security impartiality and effectiveness. Responses were aggregated by ethnicity to compute share of positive vs. negative perceptions, then tested for association using a chi‑square test.

  • Instrument: 10 statements, Likert scale.
  • Goal: Compare perception by ethnicity.
  • Limitation: Small convenience sample; results are illustrative, not definitive.

Take Action

Report, Redress, and Know Your Rights

If you experience discrimination or improper treatment at a checkpoint, you have options. Use the official channels below for redress, and explore rights guides from civil organizations. Logging details immediately (date, time, location, officers involved, witnesses) strengthens your case.

Quick FAQ

What should I document during an incident?

Record the date/time, airport, airline/flight, officer names/badge numbers, what was said/done, and any witnesses. Save boarding passes and receipts; write a brief timeline as soon as possible.

Will reporting affect my ability to fly?

Filing a complaint or redress request is your right. Submitting factual reports through official channels should not penalize lawful travelers.

How can policy improve?

Independent oversight, routine anti‑bias training (especially early on in their career), and transparent publication of screening demographics can reduce disparities while maintaining security.
